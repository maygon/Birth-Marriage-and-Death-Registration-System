BEDSIDE REGISTRATION

At the mother's bedside while she is still in the hospital, the Registrar General's Department now registers births and stillbirths. This has significantly improved the way that these important occurrences are recorded. All hospitals on the island have trained registration officers stationed there to handle these registrations. These officials work for the Agency. Important details are recorded for civil registration purposes, such as the child's date of birth, sex, mother's name, and the presence of a doctor or midwife at the time of birth. WHERE ARE BIRTHS REGISTERED?

In hospitals and birthing facilities, births are registered. Any births, deaths, and stillbirths that take place in a registration district must be registered by a Local District Registrar, who is chosen by the Registrar General. Jamaica's fourteen parishes are divided into districts, and the LDRs are typically found in the same district as or close to the districts they are in charge of.

In the district where the birth took place, it must be done. For instance, a birth that takes place in Spanish Town, St. Catherine, needs to be registered at the LDR there. A Birth Entry Number is assigned to each birth when it is registered. Each child's birth registration record is identified by this special number. The distinct entry number identifies a district's parish, its district, and the order of births that were recorded during the child's birth year.

For instance, a birth in Spanish Town, St. Catherine, would be assigned an entry number like EA 103, where "E" stands for the parish of birth, in this case, St. Catherine, and "A" stands for the district, in this case, Spanish Town.

HOW SOON AFTER BIRTH SHOULD THE EVENT BE REGISTERED?

All births must be registered:

  • Ideally within six weeks and less than one year
  • Within 14 days – Chief Resident Officer must send the notification of birth to LDR.

Parents should visit the Registration Officer for the district where the child was born and complete a Certificate of Naming if the infant is not named in the hospital.

HOSPITAL BIRTHS

The majority of births in Jamaica take place in hospitals or other birthing facilities. The parent(s)' registration process is as follows:

  • The mother, father, and child's complete information must be given. If the parents were not married at the time of the child's birth, the father must either be present to sign the birth record in person or both parents must sign and submit a declaration of paternity within six weeks of the child's birth in order for the father's name to be included.
  • If the child's name has not been decided upon at the time of hospital registration, the parent(s) will be provided a Certificate of Naming and instructions on how to complete and submit the form to the registration officer within a year. HOME/OTHER BIRTHS

Children born outside of a hospital or birthing facility must have their births documented as follows:

  • To record the birth, an informant must go to the closest Local District Registrar (LDR). This individual may be the child's parent, a witness to the birth, the resident of the home where the birth took place, or any other adult who is aware of the child's birth;
  • Within six months, or at least one year following birth, the informant must come into the LDR's office. A late registration application will be necessary after one year;
  • Similar to hospital birth registration, an "A and B registration" must be completed at the LDR if the parents are not married in order to add the father's information at the time of registration;
  • The LDR fills up the birth registration form and adds the necessary signatures;
  • Within six weeks of the registration, the original registration is sent to the Registrar General's Department's headquarters;

DEATH

It is required to categorize deaths as natural, abrupt, or violent for registration purposes. Natural deaths are ones in which the decedent was receiving regular medical care from a physician (within six weeks of death) and in which the physician has no reasonable doubt as to the cause of death.

When a person dies suddenly, it usually means that they weren't being treated by a doctor within six weeks of their passing and/or that there is some reason to suspect foul play. As the term implies, violent deaths are those that took place in violent situations.

A medical professional, medical officer, or pathologist must complete and certify one of the following for each death:

  • Medical Certificate of the Cause of Death
  • Post Mortem Examination Report
  • Certificate of Coroner
  • Coroner’s Certificate of Finding of Jury

MARRIAGE

The marriage is registered by the Civil Registrar or Marriage Officer who officiated

1. How can I become a Marriage Officer/Civil Registrar?

Ans. “All applications by ministers of religion for appointment as Marriage Officers must be in writing to the Registrar-General.” Also a checklist stating additional requirements can be located on the RGD website or you can visit any of the RGD offices.

2. What is the difference between a Marriage Officer and a Civil Registrar?

Ans. A Marriage Officer is a minister of Religion appointed to conduct marriages while “a Civil Registrar shall be ex officio a marriage officer”. You do not have to be a Minister of Religion to become a Civil Registrar however only Ministers of Religion can apply to become a Marriage Officer.

3. Should the RGD be notified when a Marriage Officer or Civil Registrar leaves the island?

Ans. Yes, the Marriage Officer is require to submit in writing of him or her leaving the island and state date of return. Also all books must be submitted to the office for safe keeping. Failure to notify the RGD when travelling overseas constitutes a breach under the Marriage Act which can result in de-gazetting.

The provision of timely data and analyses that are categorized in accordance with international standards to promote democratic planning by the government is one of the department's main goals. For the development and evaluation of national initiatives in sectors like education and health, vital statistics offer essential information. Accordingly, it is essential that the Agency not only keeps track of all life-changing occurrences (births, deaths, and marriages) that take place in Jamaica, but also makes sure that high integrity standards are upheld when gathering, compiling, and presenting such data. The cooperation of numerous groups within and connected to the Agency is necessary in order to produce timely and useful vital statistics. Local District Registrars (LDRs), who record life-changing events like births and deaths, Marriage Officers, who officiate weddings and solemnize unions, Medical Officers, who provide information from hospitals, and the Police and Courts Office Staff for Corners' cases are some of these groups.The Annual Statistical Report, which seeks to provide statistics on significant events like births, deaths, marriages, and summaries of causes of deaths, is produced and prepared for publication by the Registrar General's Department with pleasure.
